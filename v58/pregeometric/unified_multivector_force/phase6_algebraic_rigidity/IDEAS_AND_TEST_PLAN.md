# Phase 6 — Algebraic Rigidity, Multivector Closure, and the Emergence of Dimensionality

**Date**: 2026-05-19  
**Status**: Exploratory research direction following completion of Phases 1–5

## 1. Core Hypothesis (Train of Ideas)

In the pre-geometric multivector substrate, there exists an **algebraic rigidity (or closure) condition** on the protected bivector structure generated by the field. This condition is satisfied most naturally and at lowest cost when exactly three independent directions are present.

- Configurations with fewer than three independent protected directions lack sufficient "volume" or independent causal separation to support stable high-density protected modes.
- Configurations with more than three introduce extra degrees of freedom that lead to either percolation (unstable long-range connectivity), dilution of protected density, or increased "energy"/leakage cost (visible as cross-grade terms or instability in the quadratic self-interaction).
- Once a local region reaches a closed, stable configuration with exactly three independent protected directions, the dynamics of the living candidate (density sourcing via $J_\rho$, protected chiral current $J_\chi$, and the quadratic terms $\lambda \Omega^2 + \mu \langle \Omega, \Omega \rangle$ inside the safe band) self-reinforce that configuration. It becomes an attractor.

Under this view:

- The **appearance of three-dimensional space** is a *consequence* of this algebraic stability condition, not a presupposed background geometry.
- **Local constancy of $c$** (isotropic light cones) emerges because any directional bias would violate the same closure that stabilizes the three directions.
- **Preferred chirality** (why most systems "spin the same way") can arise because one handedness of the protected bivector winding satisfies the rigidity condition more easily or at lower cost than the other, or because total protected winding is (approximately) conserved across the causal web.

This is directly analogous to Kepler's realization that the ellipse is not an extra geometric law but the curve traced by a body whose angular momentum is conserved. Here, "3D + constant local $c$ + preferred handedness" would be theorems that follow from a deeper pre-geometric algebraic closure law once that law is properly expressed and coupled to the density and protection rules of the living candidate.

Two complementary mathematical routes were identified:

**(2) Multivector Trigonometry / Closure Relations**  
There exist algebraic identities (generalizations of the law of cosines, angle-sum relations, or closure conditions on the bivector subalgebra) that are satisfied without leftover grade leakage or extra cost *only* when three protected directions close on themselves. Four or more directions incur a measurable penalty in the quadratic self-interaction or in the protected density.

**(3) Extremization of Protected Density Subject to Algebraic Rigidity**  
The growth/attachment rule (and ultimately the dynamics themselves) can be understood as maximizing protected density $\rho$ (or protected density concentration) *subject to* the local protected subalgebra satisfying the rigidity/closure condition at (or near) three generators. This turns the preference for three dimensions into a variational principle on the algebra.

Routes (2) and (3) are expected to work together: the closure identity (2) defines the algebraic cost function, while the living candidate dynamics (via the quadratic terms and $f_g$) implement the extremization (3).

## 2. Why This Matters for the Program

Phases 1–5 have established:

- The living candidate produces self-stabilizing attractors on relational graphs.
- These attractors are robust and resilient to small perturbations (Phase 4).
- The quadratic self-interaction and ambient modulation $f_g$ are necessary for that stability (Phase 5).
- Internal observers can reconstruct geometry consistent with global maps (Phase 3).

However, the measured effective dimensionality in all runs has remained stubbornly sub-3 (typically 1.0–1.8). This suggests that the current discretization and implementation of protected chirality does not yet fully capture the "rigidity at exactly three directions" condition. The algebraic closure mechanism proposed here is a candidate for the missing ingredient that would push the stable attractor toward a robust ~3.

If successful, this would provide a pre-geometric explanation for why three spatial dimensions are special, without ever presupposing a 3-dimensional manifold.

## 3. Proposed Testing Strategy

### 3.1 Numerical Tests (Python / Relational Graphs)

**Define a local rigidity / closure cost**

At each node $x$, extract the protected bivector components of the local state $M(x)$ or the generated $\Omega(x)$. Compute:

- $r(x)$ = effective number (rank) of independent protected directions.
- A closure defect $C_{\rm defect}(x)$ measuring failure of algebraic closure (e.g., norm of associator, commutator, or deviation from a proposed multivector trigonometric identity).
- A combined rigidity cost, for example:

  $$
  C_{\rm rig}(x) = \alpha \cdot \phi(r(x)) + \beta \cdot C_{\rm defect}(x)
  $$

  where $\phi(r) = (r-3)^2$ (or a soft version that also penalizes $r < 3$).

**Modify the growth / attachment rule**

Alter the existing density-biased or $\Omega$-feedback attachment probability to include the rigidity cost:

$$
\text{activity}(x) = \rho(x) \cdot \exp(-C_{\rm rig}(x))
$$

(or an equivalent form that favors low rigidity cost).

Optionally, make the quadratic self-interaction strength itself depend on $C_{\rm rig}$ (a first embodiment of route (2)).

**Experiments**

- Re-run the 500–600+ node protocol from Phase 4 with several values of the weighting parameters $\alpha, \beta$.
- Measure and compare to Phase 4 baselines:
  - Late-time mean and variance of $d_{\rm eff}$
  - Protected density concentration and its stability
  - Perturbation recovery metrics (relaxation time, final deviations)
  - Histogram of local $r(x)$ across high-density nodes (does a peak appear at exactly 3?)
- If the attractor moves upward toward 2.5–3.0 with improved stability metrics, this constitutes positive evidence.

All experiments must use the **exact** locked living candidate parameters when not performing deliberate ablations.

### 3.2 Formal Tests (Lean)

**Define abstract structures**

- `ProtectedBivSubalgebra` — a structure representing the algebra generated by the protected bivector components at a node.
- `rigidity_cost : ProtectedBivSubalgebra → ℝ` (or an ordered type) implementing the algebraic cost defined above.
- `IsRigidAt3 (A : ProtectedBivSubalgebra) : Prop` — the predicate that the cost is minimized (or below threshold) at exactly three generators with vanishing closure defect.

**State conjectures linking the living candidate to rigidity**

```lean
-- The living candidate dynamics tend to drive nodes toward configurations
-- where the protected subalgebra satisfies IsRigidAt3
theorem living_candidate_tends_to_rigid_at_3
    (graph : RelationalGraph)
    (h : EvolvesUnderLivingCandidate graph) :
    ∀ x, Tendsto (rigidity_cost (protectedSubalgebraAt x)) (IsRigidAt3 ...)
```

On finite models, prove or computationally verify that nodes with high protected density after evolution have low rigidity cost and satisfy `IsRigidAt3`.

**Connect to existing theorems**

Show that the already-certified monotonicity (`causal_ball_size_monotonic`) and stability results become stronger, or hold with better bounds, when the rigidity cost is taken into account. This would demonstrate that the algebraic closure condition is not an extra assumption but is already latent in the living candidate dynamics.

## 4. Concrete First Implementation Steps

**Python side (in `phase6_algebraic_rigidity/` or by extending `phase4_stability_and_self_regulation/stability_dynamics.py`)**

1. Add a function `local_protected_rank_and_closure_defect(node)` operating on the bivector part of `node.omega_coeffs` or `node.coeffs`.
2. Implement a simple but non-trivial `rigidity_cost`.
3. Modify the attachment / living-activity function to include the cost (with tunable $\alpha, \beta$).
4. Add logging of the distribution of $r(x)$ and $C_{\rm rig}(x)$ over time.
5. Run controlled experiments comparing against the Phase 4 baseline (same seeds, same number of nodes, same perturbation protocol).
6. Export the final graphs with the new per-node rigidity data for Lean ingestion.

**Lean side (new file `lean/UnifiedMultivector/Phase6Rigidity.lean`)**

1. Define `ProtectedBivSubalgebra` (initially as a record containing the relevant bivector coefficients).
2. Port a computable version of `rigidity_cost`.
3. Define `IsRigidAt3`.
4. State the main conjecture `living_candidate_tends_to_rigid_at_3` (or a weaker finite version).
5. On the exported snapshots from the Python experiments, computationally verify the conjecture for the concrete graphs.
6. Attempt to prove (or at least state cleanly) that the existing stability theorems are strengthened when rigidity is enforced.

**Success criteria for the first iteration**

- The numerical attractor shows a measurable upward shift in mean $d_{\rm eff}$ and/or a reduction in variance compared to Phase 4.
- The Lean side can at least evaluate `rigidity_cost` on real exported nodes and confirm a correlation between low cost and high protected density.
- Any improvement in stability or dimensionality is reproducible across multiple seeds.

If the first simple implementation shows no improvement, the functional form of the rigidity cost (or the way "independent directions" are counted) must be refined — this would itself be valuable information about what the correct multivector-trigonometric closure relation should look like.

## 5. Relation to the Living Candidate

The quadratic self-interaction term $\lambda \Omega^2 + \mu \langle \Omega, \Omega \rangle$ is the most natural place for an algebraic rigidity penalty to appear, because it already acts on the bivector grades that carry the protected directions. The ambient modulation $f_g(\rho)$ provides the global feedback that can penalize regions whose local algebra is far from the rigid value, thereby favoring growth in regions that are already close to three closed directions.

If the rigidity cost can be expressed as a function of the local bivector subalgebra, it may be possible to absorb (part of) it into a refined version of the living candidate itself, rather than adding it as an external rule. This would be the cleanest outcome: the preference for three dimensions would be an intrinsic consequence of the equation rather than an extra postulate.
