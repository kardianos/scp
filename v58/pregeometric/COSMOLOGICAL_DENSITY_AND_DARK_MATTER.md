# Cosmological Density and the Dark Matter Problem

**Date**: 2026-05-18  
**Status**: Conceptual theory note. Explores whether the first-principles hypothesis (gravity as an emergent effect of gradients in the multivector field density) can account for the observed extra gravitational effects on galactic and cosmological scales without introducing new particle species. This is a direct cosmological extension of the ideas in `MULTIVECTOR_DENSITY_GRAVITY.md` and `MEDIUM_STATE_WAVES.md`.

## 1. The Core Intuition

Standard cosmology infers a large "missing mass" component because the gravitational effect (rotation curves, weak lensing, cluster dynamics, structure growth) is stronger than what the visible baryonic mass can produce under Newtonian gravity or general relativity.

In the v58 framework the only fundamental object is the multivector field \( M \). "Mass" is a localized elevation of its density invariant:

\[
\rho_M = \frac12 \big( M \tilde{M} - v^2 \big)
\]

(or the equivalent expression after projection onto the vacuum manifold).

The user's suggestion is: what if, on scales of galaxies and larger, the background value of the multivector field itself is not uniform? What if galaxies and clusters sit inside extended regions of mildly elevated field density? Then the total gravitational sourcing at large radii would include contributions from this extended medium, and the "extra mass" would be an intrinsic property of the field configuration rather than unseen particles.

This is not a new particle. It is the same object that already produces baryonic gravity, gravitational waves (as collective excitations of the medium state), and the effective geometry experienced by all fluctuations.

## 2. How the Multivector Density Picture Produces "Extra Gravity"

From `MULTIVECTOR_DENSITY_GRAVITY.md`, in the Newtonian limit the effective potential (or the force-generating bivector \(\omega\)) is sourced by the *total* density deviation of the multivector field, not only the sharply peaked baryonic component.

At a point \(\mathbf{x}\) the effective gravitational potential receives contributions from the entire distribution:

\[
\Phi(\mathbf{x}) \propto \int \frac{\rho_M(\mathbf{x}')}{|\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{x}'|} \, d^3x'
\]

We can decompose the density field into two parts:

\[
\rho_M(\mathbf{x}) = \rho_{\text{baryons}}(\mathbf{x}) + \delta\rho_{\text{medium}}(\mathbf{x})
\]

where \(\rho_{\text{baryons}}\) is sharply peaked on stars and gas, and \(\delta\rho_{\text{medium}}\) is a smoother, more extended component that may vary on scales of 10–100 kpc or even Mpc.

An observer who assumes all gravitational sourcing comes from visible baryons will interpret the extra effect of \(\delta\rho_{\text{medium}}\) as "dark matter mass" inside radius \(r\):

\[
M_{\text{DM, inferred}}(r) \approx \int_0^r 4\pi r'^2 \, \delta\rho_{\text{medium}}(r') \, dr'
\]

If \(\delta\rho_{\text{medium}}(r)\) falls off much more slowly than the baryonic surface density (for example, roughly constant or \(\propto 1/r\)), the rotation curve \(v^2(r) = r \partial_r \Phi\) remains flat at large radii even though the visible mass is centrally concentrated.

This is the mechanism by which the theory can reproduce the phenomenological success of dark matter halos while using only the single multivector field.

## 3. Cosmological Version

On still larger scales the same logic applies. If the multivector field has long-range correlations or slow spatial variations (perhaps inherited from early-universe conditions or from its own collective dynamics), then the effective gravitational clustering on Mpc scales receives an extra contribution from the medium density field. Structure formation would then proceed with a total source term that includes both baryonic peaks and this smoother background component.

The "dark matter" in this picture is the large-scale, relatively smooth part of \(\delta\rho_{\text{medium}}\) that is not locked one-to-one with the visible galaxies.

## 4. Proposed Equation (Sketch of the Extended Derivation)

In the Newtonian limit of the multivector formulation, the effective mass that enters the Poisson equation at galactic radii is:

\[
M_{\text{eff}}(r) = M_{\text{baryons}}(<r) + 4\pi \int_0^r r'^2 \, \delta\rho_{\text{medium}}(r') \, dr'
\]

where the second term is the cumulative contribution of the extended medium density.

For weak lensing the same \(M_{\text{eff}}\) enters the deflection angle because light follows null geodesics in the effective geometry generated by the total density field (the same geometry that produces the orbital dynamics of stars and gas).

In the full multivector language (before the Newtonian limit) the bivector \(\omega\) at a point is generated by the geometric derivative of the entire multivector configuration:

\[
\omega(\mathbf{x}) \propto \int \mathcal{K}(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{x}') \, \nabla' M(\mathbf{x}') \tilde{M}(\mathbf{x}') \, dV'
\]

where \(\mathcal{K}\) is the appropriate Green's function of the algebra. Both the sharply peaked baryonic deviations and the smoother medium variations contribute to \(\omega\), and therefore to the force felt by test excitations and to the curvature felt by light.

This is the direct translation of "gravity as density" into the multivector formalism.

## 5. The Three Hard Observational Problems

The user's intuition that "lensing happens automatically because gravity = density of the field" is partially correct but incomplete. The subtlety lies in the *dynamics and spatial distribution* of \(\delta\rho_{\text{medium}}\), not in the geometric statement itself. Below we examine the three classic challenges.

### 5.1 The Bullet Cluster (and other merging clusters)

**Observation**: In the Bullet Cluster (1E 0657-56) the weak-lensing mass peaks are spatially offset from the X-ray emitting baryonic gas. The lensing signal (total gravitational effect) does not follow the visible baryons after the collision.

**In a pure density picture**: For the lensing mass to be separated from the gas, the extended medium density \(\delta\rho_{\text{medium}}\) must itself be able to lag behind or be stripped differently from the baryonic plasma during a high-speed merger.

This is possible *in principle* if the multivector medium state has some independent dynamical degree of freedom or memory (it is not slaved instantaneously to the local baryon density). However, it is not automatic. If \(\delta\rho_{\text{medium}}\) is generated solely by and perfectly tracks the baryons at all times, the lensing peaks would stay with the gas and the Bullet Cluster would be a falsification.

The requirement is therefore that the medium state must be able to develop its own large-scale structure and to respond to collisions with some inertia or viscosity of its own. This is a non-trivial dynamical constraint on any concrete realization of the multivector field or pre-geometric substrate.

### 5.2 Gravitational Lensing versus Orbital Dynamics

**Observation**: In general relativity the same metric tensor governs both null geodesics (light deflection, strong lensing) and timelike geodesics (stellar orbits, galaxy dynamics). Any successful theory must reproduce this universality to high precision.

**In the density picture**: Because both light and massive particles are excitations (or collective modes) of the *same* multivector field, they both feel the geometry generated by the total \(\rho_M\) (baryons + medium). In that sense the user's intuition is correct: once the effective geometry is defined by the medium state, lensing and dynamics are automatically described by the same object.

The remaining subtlety is *how* the medium state couples to different excitations. If the coupling were species-dependent (e.g., the medium density affects massive particles more strongly than photons), universality would be violated. The first-principles requirement in `EXPECTED_BEHAVIOR.md` §3.1 and §4 explicitly demands that the mapping from medium state to effective metric must be universal and geometric for all small fluctuations. Any concrete model must enforce this, not assume it.

### 5.3 CMB Power Spectrum, Baryon Acoustic Oscillations, and Structure Growth

**Observation**: The angular power spectrum of the cosmic microwave background, the scale of baryon acoustic oscillations, and the growth rate of large-scale structure all require a dominant, pressureless, clustering component that was already present at recombination and that clusters efficiently on small scales while remaining relatively smooth on the largest scales.

**In the density picture**: The extended medium density \(\delta\rho_{\text{medium}}\) must play exactly this role. It must:
- Have been sufficiently clustered at early times to source the observed CMB anisotropies,
- Have the right sound speed (near zero) so that it does not erase the BAO feature,
- Grow under its own self-gravity in a way that matches the observed galaxy clustering and weak-lensing power spectrum.

If \(\delta\rho_{\text{medium}}\) is too tightly locked to the baryons, it will not provide enough early gravitational potential wells. If it is completely decoupled, it may produce the wrong ratio of baryonic to total clustering or violate the observed baryon fraction in clusters.

Again, the geometry itself is generated by the total density, so lensing and dynamics remain consistent; the hard part is getting the *dynamics and initial conditions* of the medium state right on cosmological scales.

## 6. Summary of the Current Conceptual Status

The multivector density picture offers a coherent mechanism by which the "extra gravity" attributed to dark matter can emerge from the same field whose localized excitations are the baryons. The equation

\[
M_{\text{eff}}(r) = M_{\text{baryons}}(<r) + \int \delta\rho_{\text{medium}}
\]

is the direct translation of that idea into the Newtonian limit of the formalism developed in `MULTIVECTOR_DENSITY_GRAVITY.md`.

The three classic observational problems do not disappear simply because "gravity is density." They reappear as requirements on the *spatial distribution and independent dynamics* of the extended medium density component. The geometric universality of lensing and dynamics is largely automatic once the effective geometry is defined by the total multivector state; the difficult work lies in the clustering history and collision response of that state.

This line of thought is therefore promising but still at the level of a target phenomenology. Any concrete pre-geometric substrate or multivector dynamics must eventually demonstrate that it can produce a \(\delta\rho_{\text{medium}}\) component with the required radial profiles and dynamical behavior while still satisfying the eight requirements listed in `EXPECTED_BEHAVIOR.md` §4.

A more speculative extension — exploring what it would mean for the multivector medium to develop its own "fluid-like" structures (pressure heads, outbound tails, wakes, and lag during motion or collisions) — is developed in the companion note `MEDIUM_DYNAMICS_TAILS_AND_WAKES.md`. That note treats these dynamical features as a possible way to give the extended density the independent memory and response needed for the Bullet Cluster and similar observations.
