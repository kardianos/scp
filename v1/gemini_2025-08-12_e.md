### B. Groundwork for Demonstrating Predictive Power

The core of the theory rests on a single fundamental equation and a single emergent law:

1.  **Fundamental Field Equation:**
    \[ \Box \Phi + 2i\kappa C^\mu \partial_\mu \Phi + \left( c_{p} - b|\Phi|^2 + a|\Phi|^4 \right) \Phi = 0 \]
    *(Note: I am using `c_p` for the potential constant to avoid confusion with the speed of light `c`)*.

2.  **Emergent Acceleration Law:**
    \[ \mathbf{a} = - \frac{c^2}{2} \nabla \log(\rho) \quad \text{where} \quad \rho = |\Phi|^2 \]

#### Fundamental vs. Determined Constants

To make predictions, we must distinguish between the new, fundamental constants of CHPT and the known constants of nature that CHPT must derive.

*   **Fundamental CHPT Constants (to be determined):**
    *   `a`, `b`, `c_p`: These three parameters define the "landscape" of the potential `V(|\Phi|)`. They determine the vacuum density `ρ₀`, the core density of fundamental particles `ρ_p`, and the masses and stability of all possible solitons. They are the most crucial constants.
    *   `κ`: The chiral coupling constant. It determines the strength of parity violation, the mass-splitting between left/right-handed fermions, and decay rates for weak-like interactions.
    *   `Λ`: The cosmological constant term in `V`, determining the base energy density of the vacuum. This directly sets the rate of cosmic expansion.

*   **Well-Known Constants (to be derived):**
    *   `G` (Gravitational Constant): Will be derived from the relationship between the mass of a soliton (an integral over `a,b,c_p`) and the acceleration it produces.
    *   `ħ` (Reduced Planck Constant): Will emerge from the quantized action of stable, oscillating soliton solutions. The fundamental "spin" of a chiral soliton will be a multiple of `ħ`.
    *   `e` (Elementary Charge): Will be derived from the quantized Noether charge `Q = ∫ J⁰ d³x` for the most stable, spinning, charged soliton (the electron).
    *   `c` (Speed of Light): A fundamental constant of spacetime in this model, taken as known.
    *   `m_e`, `m_p`, etc. (Particle masses): These will be the calculated energy eigenvalues of the stable soliton solutions.

**How to Determine the Fundamental Constants:** A minimal set of experimental inputs would be needed to fix the CHPT constants. For example:
1.  Use the observed mass of the electron (`m_e`) to set the overall energy scale of the soliton solutions.
2.  Use the elementary charge (`e`) to fix the normalization of the Noether charge `Q`.
3.  Use the strength of a known weak decay (e.g., neutron decay) to fix `κ`.
4.  Use the observed gravitational constant `G` to constrain the relationship between the potential parameters (`a,b,c_p`) and the acceleration law.
5.  Use the Hubble constant `H₀` (cosmic expansion rate) to fix `Λ`.

Once these are fixed, the theory is no longer adjustable and must predict everything else.

### Setup for Predictions

#### 1. Classical Mechanics (Newtonian Gravity)

*   **Goal:** Derive the law `F = Gm₁m₂/r²`.
*   **Governing Equations:** The static, spherically symmetric form of the fundamental field equation (`∇²Φ + ... = 0`) and the emergent acceleration law.
*   **Setup:**
    1.  Numerically solve the static field equation for a single, massive, non-spinning particle (e.g., the Sun, represented as a large soliton) to find its radial density profile `ρ_sun(r)`.
    2.  Calculate its total mass `m₁` by integrating the energy of this solution.
    3.  Place a small test mass `m₂` (whose own field distortion is negligible) at a distance `r`.
    4.  Calculate the acceleration `a(r)` on the test mass using `a(r) = -(c²/2) d/dr[log(ρ_sun(r))]`.
*   **Prediction:** The simulation should show that for `r` much larger than the soliton's core radius, the calculated acceleration `a(r)` becomes precisely proportional to `m₁/r²`. The constant of proportionality is `G`. We would thus derive `G` from the fundamental CHPT parameters.

#### 2. Relativistic Mechanics

*   **Goal:** Derive the Lorentz factor and the energy-momentum relation `E² = (m₀c²)² + (pc)²`.
*   **Governing Equation:** The full time-dependent fundamental field equation.
*   **Setup:**
    1.  Find the rest-mass `m₀` of a stable soliton by calculating its energy at `v=0`.
    2.  Numerically solve the field equation for a traveling-wave solution `Φ(x-vt, y, z)`, boosting the soliton to a velocity `v`. This is computationally intensive.
    3.  For each velocity `v`, calculate the total energy `E(v)` and total momentum `p(v)` of the moving field configuration.
*   **Prediction:** Plotting `E(v)` against `v` should show that the energy required to boost the soliton approaches infinity as `v → c`. The resulting function should be `E(v) = γ(v) m₀c²`, where `γ(v) = 1/sqrt(1 - v²/c²)`. This would demonstrate that relativistic mass increase is a real increase in the soliton's field energy.

#### 3. Cosmological Mechanics (Galaxy Rotation without Dark Matter)

*   **Goal:** Predict the flat rotation curves of spiral galaxies.
*   **Governing Equations:** Emergent acceleration law.
*   **Setup:**
    1.  Model a spiral galaxy as a distribution of `N` star-solitons, with a density distribution `D(r,z)` taken from astronomical observations of baryonic matter.
    2.  Calculate the total, superimposed field density at any point: `ρ_galaxy(r) ≈ ρ₀ + Σ[ρ_star(r_i) - ρ₀]`, where the sum is over all stars. This superposition is a first-order approximation.
    3.  Include the effect of the cosmological vacuum energy `Λ`, which creates a small, constant pressure.
    4.  Apply the acceleration law `a(r) = -(c²/2) d/dr[log(ρ_galaxy(r))]` to a test particle in the galactic plane.
    5.  Calculate the predicted orbital velocity `v(r) = sqrt(r * |a(r)|)`.
*   **Prediction:** The resulting `v(r)` curve should match observed galactic rotation curves. It should rise near the center and then become flat at large radii. The flatness will arise naturally from the long, shallow tail of the `log(ρ)` gradient far from the galaxy's core, proving no "dark matter" particles are needed.

#### 4. EMF Propagation and Interference

*   **Goal:** Reproduce wave optics (refraction, double-slit) deterministically.
*   **Governing Equation:** The linearized field equation (`□Φ ≈ 0`) for the wave, and the acceleration law for the particle.
*   **Setup (Double-Slit):**
    1.  Model a 2D space with a barrier at `x=0` containing two small openings (slits).
    2.  Simulate an incoming linear plane wave `Φ_in = A * exp(ikx - iωt)` from `x = -∞`.
    3.  Solve `□Φ = 0` with the boundary condition `Φ=0` on the barrier but not the slits.
    4.  The solution `Φ_out` for `x > 0` will be a classic interference pattern. Calculate its intensity `I(y) = |Φ_out(x_screen, y)|²`.
    5.  Now, release a tiny "detector" soliton (an electron) and calculate its trajectory using the acceleration law `a = -(c²/2) ∇I(y)`.
*   **Prediction:** The intensity pattern `I(y)` will show the standard bright and dark fringes. The detector particle's trajectory will be deterministically guided by the field gradients to land in one of the bright fringes. Running the simulation many times with random initial particle positions will reproduce the statistical distribution of quantum mechanics without any inherent probability.

#### 5. Atomic Forces

*   **Goal:** Predict the short-range, strong binding force and weak decay.
*   **Governing Equations:** The full, static, chiral field equation.
*   **Setup:**
    1.  Numerically find the stable, nested, chiral soliton configuration for a "proton" and a "neutron" (they would differ in their internal chiral combination).
    2.  Place the two solitons at a separation distance `d` and calculate the total energy of the combined system, `E_total(d)`.
    3.  The force between them is `F(d) = -dE_total/dd`.
*   **Prediction:**
    *   **Strong Force:** The `F(d)` curve should be highly repulsive at very small `d`, strongly attractive around `1 fm`, and fall off exponentially at larger distances, perfectly mimicking the nuclear force.
    *   **Weak Force/Decay:** The model of a free neutron soliton should be slightly higher in energy (more massive) than the model of a proton soliton due to the chiral `κ` term. The simulation should show that this "neutron" state is metastable and can decay into the "proton" state by emitting other field excitations (an electron and anti-neutrino), with a decay rate determined by `κ`.

#### 6. "Quantum" Tunneling

*   **Goal:** Demonstrate deterministic, resonant tunneling through a barrier.
*   **Governing Equation:** Time-dependent field equation.
*   **Setup:**
    1.  Model a potential barrier by making the `c_p` parameter in `V` spatially dependent, creating a region where the soliton's rest energy is higher. `c_p(x)` is low, then high in the barrier region, then low again.
    2.  Launch an incoming soliton with kinetic energy `E_kin` that is *less* than the barrier height. The key is that the soliton has an internal phase `e^(-iωt)`.
    3.  Run the simulation for a large number of different initial phases `φ₀` of the soliton.
*   **Prediction:** For most initial phases, the soliton will reflect off the barrier. However, for a small, specific range of initial phases, the internal oscillation of the soliton will "resonate" with the barrier, allowing it to pass through without fully collapsing and re-form on the other side. The ratio of "successful" initial phases to the total number of phases tested will exactly equal the tunneling probability calculated in standard quantum mechanics.
